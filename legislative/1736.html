Statement

I was very happy that the voters of Colorado approved anti-gerrymandering with Amendments Y and Z! These amendments seem to be such common sense, which I like to believe my home state prides itself in. What a nonsensical and unfair system to have the party in power at the time right after the census to be able to affect elections for the next 10 years (until the next census)! We are a proud purple state (more blue now, but still with plenty of red) and I truly want everyone's vote to count. While surely the commission process won't be perfect, I appreciate that there are definite criteria for creating districts and that redistricting will follow these definite criteria to try to make districts more "rational" and less political. Obviously, with Colorado picking up a new House seat soon, redistricting is all the more important this time around.

Statement on Working with Consensus

I have always been a consensus-builder, first in my family, then with my friends, then in school politics and then in the workplace. I try hard at understanding the other point of view when a situation is contentious. Further, I look out for the people who aren't being heard and loop them back into the conversation. I understand that each criteria in forming a district is not completely concrete, but these criteria aren't entirely squishy either. This will help tremendously in deflecting personal opinion and stances, I believe. Further, you are looking for people to serve on this commission who appreciate the process and believe, fundamentally, that no matter what, districting should be impartial and that the voters of Colorado who approved Amendments Y and Z have asked this commission to make this happen! Will it be completely painless for me to know that certain districts are strongly red and will remain so? No; however, that doesn't mean I am not committed to a fair and impartial process. I am completely committed to that. Here is one example that explains my ability to be impartial: as a reference librarian, I never comment or try to influence what someone is wanting to research; if the person wants to find out about the hunting seasons for quail, turkey and elk in Colorado, as well as which animals you can hunt with a crossbow or rifle, I find that information and deliver it to them in a professional, friendly manner. That I am a vegetarian, don't like hunting and don't like guns or crossbows has absolutely no bearing on how I treat the person or the work.

Relevant Analytic Skills for Commission

I love using disparate pieces of information to get a complete picture of a situation.  This is one of the reasons that I'm a reference librarian... I want to complete the picture for patrons with complex problems (and I get to learn in the process, which enjoy).  I am a great listener and ask good questions. I also seek the holes in arguments and can poke at them (in a very diplomatic way).  On a different note, I am not afraid to say that I don't understand something or that I need clarification; in other words, my ego does not get in the way of asking experts.  I live my life naturally analyzing complex situations; that is what has kept me happy cognitively while mostly focusing on being a mom.  Most recently I answered this complex question: Would the use of dry hydrogen peroxide technology in HVAC filtering systems be a feasible way to assure jittery teachers and get the most vulnerable students back to in-person learning in public school? After reading extensively, asking many questions, consulting experts, working with different stakeholders and considering a bunch of variables, I, along with state and local health officials and local school officials came to the conclusion that it was not feasible during this part of the pandemic.
