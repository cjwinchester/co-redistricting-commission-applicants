I advocated for the accurate counting of historically under-represented areas, and I worked hard to get as many people as possible counted in the timeframe allowed. This was non-partisan work. As a water scientist and well owner in Colorado, I have an interest in, and have considered others’ interests in, Colorado’s water resources. I have attended many water conservation district and water user association meetings, and I have presented my research before some of these groups.

Statement

My PhD work at Oxford brought me in 2014 to the San Luis Valley. In southern Colorado I spent several years studying precipitation patterns, groundwater chemistry, water flowpaths, and by default—water law. I learned all of these are a changing landscape influenced by human settlement, which in turn, is dependent on physical geography. I saw how physical geography and human geography interact. In 2020, I had the opportunity to work for the US Census. While I had previously been involved in my community as a board member for the Colorado Field Institute and had actively participated in water board and water association meetings, the Census offered me my first experience linking community involvement with state and federal representation. To me, it was the clearest demonstration of “democracy in action.” The phrase “democracy in action” is often applied to the simple act of voting. No doubt, voting is important. Our country just experienced an election with its highest voter turnout in history. But our Constitution provides for an electoral college system, and the number of Congressional representatives as well as the boundaries of a state’s congressional and legislative districts are all based on data ascertained by the US Census Bureau. In 2018, Colorado passed laws providing for a team of independent commissioners tasked with re-drawing district boundaries every ten years based on the most recent Census data. I would like to follow the US Census data for Colorado I helped gather and spent time analyzing. Its next point of usefulness will be to help Colorado reframe its new congressional and legislative boundaries based on changes in settlement patterns and population data. I want to use the knowledge and skills I’ve acquired to help determine the map boundaries for Colorado. I want to actively participate in my state’s democratic process. I want to learn more about this process by getting involved directly. Because I am a geographer who loves maps, joining the redistricting commission seems an appropriate and important way for me to serve my community, state, and country.
