Organization and Advocacy Experience

The last 22 years of my law practice have focused on the advocacy for the rights of federal employees who believe they have suffered discrimination. I have tried and won or resolved short of litigation cases for numerous women who suffered sexual harassment in the workplace, an HIV-positive male who worked the US Army in Colorado Springs, an African-American USPS worker, a white male at the end of his long career for a federal scientific agency in the Boulder area, a cancer survivor and the only woman on a Denver-based senior management team, to name just a few. Not only did I listen to and advocate for those without a voice in the federal workplace, I participated in the leadership of the Colorado Plaintiff's Employment Law Association for many years and remain a member today. This organization promotes and increases public awareness of employee rights and provides educational opportunities, information and assistance to attorney who represent employees. The most recent success of Colorado PELA was advocacy for Proposition 118, Paid Family and Medical Leave which the voters approved on November 3, 2020.

Statement

As I contemplate the end of my legal career, I long to return to community service. My term as a Hearing Officer for the Denver Civil Service Commission ended in 2012. For five years, I heard cases of Denver police officers disciplinary appeals either as a sole officer or as a panel member. Everyone should serve a greater cause and benefit. Sitting on this commission would provide me the opportunity to serve the greater community, to give back, and assure those often without a voice receive representation.

Statement on Working with Consensus

As I wrote, I served as a Denver Civil Service Commission Hearing Officer most often as a member of three-person panel. Likewise, I served on arbitration panels for the National Association of Security Dealers, now FINRA. Therefore, I have often heard evidence with two others and then come to a consensus on disputed matters of police discipline and securities claims. In all my years hearing these distinct types of cases, I do not recall any split decisions on panels on which I served. I have also mediated numerous cases to settlements for clients I represented. No doubt in mediation, finding the middle ground between parties while not discounting the value of my client's case will serve me well as a member of the commission. I once heard a mediator/settlement judge say that if both parties feel they have given up too much, perhaps the settlement was a good one. I have relied on that statement in subsequent matters many times. Finally, I have since 1999, worked as an independent contractor for some federal agencies investigating over 100 claims of workplace discrimination. In each of thee claims I review initial stated claims with supporting documents, select and then interview relevant witnesses, and gather additional documentary evidence to compile a report for the decision maker. This work requires me to be fair to both sides, the claimant and the agency, and make no statement as to the validity of the claims. Accordingly, via this investigation work, I have honed my skills in fairness, impartiality, and listening.

Relevant Analytic Skills for Commission

I have practiced law now for some 33 years and tried numerous cases. Thus, my ability to apply logic and reason to difficult problems has served me well in various forums. From an initial consultation with a prospective client, I have analyzed the events the person described, reviewed documents, and had to make determinations whether, not only, if the person has a claim, but also whether the claim was one in which an administrative body or a court would award damages. Accordingly, problem solving through logic and reason has been an ultimate goal in my litigation practice.
