Statement

I believe a democratized process for districting, for the people of Colorado and by the people of Colorado, ensures fairness. It also allows for greater transparency and encourages civic engagement.

Statement on Working with Consensus

Colorado just had one of the highest turnouts in voting history as a state. There is massive civic involvement in this state, and it is important to ensure all voices are heard. I have spent a substantial part of my career being a civic servant or supporting civic engagement through educating students and community members, working alongside neighborhood committees, or pounding pavement to influence folks one door knock at a time. Furthermore, while I may be a registered Democrat, I do not vote party line. I also read the blue book back to front and consider implications of TABOR, Gallagher, and other amendments. I also think deeply about implications of an amendment versus a proposition. Every vote inside of those decisions matters. Therefore, the geography of those votes matter and designing equity and fairness within that geography is critical. How equity and fairness playout takes dialogue, compromise, and the ability to see myriad vantage points to what makes sense. As a life long educator, a large part of my responsibility is to deeply listen to my community, understanding tension, complexity, and nuance. Everyone is different, and we can only co-exist when we begin to understand one another, begin to empathize, and begin to move forward together.

Relevant Analytic Skills for Commission

In my career I have written policy memos, designed criteria for weighing alternatives, and applied counterfactuals in order to critically examine both the validity of the frameworks being used as well as potential solutions. I have also had to do this work inside the context of building broad and diverse coalitions, especially when working with school board members, district leaders, and school building leaders when considering ways to implement policies across the district.
