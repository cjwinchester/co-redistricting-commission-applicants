Statement on Working with Consensus

A realistic, though often unspoken, truth of nonprofits is the the mentality of scarcity. The idea that if one program wins a grant, that means there is less money for my program to do so. This can create a toxic environment with good, well-intentioned people fighting for the same resources. In my time as a director of College Track, I was faced the challenge of balancing my own programs metrics and goals with the fact that my program's success would likely take away from others. I could have operated from a place of hoarding contacts, relationships, and building barriers for communication, but instead, I worked with as many people as I could. I worked with people whose goals were in direct opposition to mine, I worked with people who would potentially take students out of my program which would jeopardize my funding. I worked to center each conversation around what is the best outcome we can achieve? That question was not couched in best outcome for me, or the best outcome for one program, but what was the best outcome. My ability to keep a vision 5 years out, 10 years out, and beyond helps me remain balanced. I believe that mentality, that sense of teamwork, helped boost the success of not only my program, but the students we served and the other nonprofits in the same building as well.
