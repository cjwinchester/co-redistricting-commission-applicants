Organization and Advocacy Experience

I’ve spent my professional career advocating for public lands conservation in Colorado and across the Rocky Mountain West, helping resource-based economies diversify their economies and avoid boom-and-bust cycles. The work requires building coalitions across diverse interests groups who might have very different backgrounds – but so often share common interests.

Statement

Guaranteeing fair representation for all Coloradans is fundamental to maintaining a healthy and functioning Democracy. I hope to serve on the commission to provide a voice for the rural West Slope. As someone with two young kids, who’s raising a family in western Colorado, I feel an extra sense of urgency to ensure that Colorado’s political districts are fairly drawn and represent each and every Colorado resident – and not drawn to help only a small segment of our state who might be better connected or more skilled at influencing the process.

Statement on Working with Consensus

People often have fierce differences of opinions. But being honest and up-front, listening to multiple perspectives – and most importantly – making sure that a broad-set of diverse voices are being consulted, is the best way to find common ground and come to an impartial decision. When decisions are informed only by a small set of individuals, whether they have ulterior motives or not, it becomes virtually impossible to come to a fair outcome. That’s true in any process, whether you’re drawing political boundaries or developing policy priorities for an organization to advocate for. This is an approach I’ve tried to bring to my career and projects I have worked on. As one example, over the last six months, I’ve led a project convening multiple tribal nations to better understand tribal nations’ unique perspectives on conservation. The outcome of the project is a ‘statement of principles’ for policy makers to better integrate indigenous perspectives when formulating conservation policy, including funding for land management and new protected areas.

Relevant Analytic Skills for Commission

Across my academic and professional career, I’ve acquired a multitude of analysis skills which I can employ as a commissioner, from data collection and analysis to project management and strategic planning. For example: • Project management: I currently oversee and manage a project with a budget of $1 million per year, strategically deploying resources to support policy advocacy efforts at the federal level. I conceive of tactics, build partners and coalitions, and support conservation policy efforts. • Data analysis: I have deep experience synthesizing publicly-available government data – from public lands visitation data to oil royalty payments – building advocacy campaigns around the data to support policy efforts. • Consensus-building: I am skilled at building relationships across organizations, understanding the unique perspectives of individuals and groups. I’m also skilled at negotiation, having convinced two toddlers of the virtues of sleeping through the night in their own bed.
