I develop facts and apply them to law, but I remain open to different perspectives, as well.

Relevant Analytic Skills for Commission

I joke that they beat the creativity out of you in law school, and while I say it tongue-in-cheek, there is still a little truth in that statement. Lawyers are trained to analyze issues in a specific, formalized way. We identify the specific issue in question, develop the relevant facts, apply them to applicable law, and reach a reasoned conclusion. This is called the "IRAC" method. Each step in this analysis is important. If the issue is framed too generally or too specifically or not quite on point, for example, the facts, law, and conclusion will be disconnected from the question presented. The same applies to which facts are "relevant" and which law is "applicable." After 17 years as a trial and appellate lawyer, I have learned to be precise about these kinds of decisions and analyses. That said, all of this analytical ability would be effectively worthless if one did not also have the ability to communicate clearly with people from all backgrounds and to ensure that the views of others are heard and that they are valued, as well. I have broad life experience: I have lived overseas, worked every kind of odd job, served in the military, been a police officer, owned a business, and been and a spouse and a parent. I believe this broad experience helps me relate to, communicate with, and understand the perspectives of a broad array of people.
