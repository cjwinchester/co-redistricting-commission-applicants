Statement on Working with Consensus

As an employee of the National Renewable Energy Laboratory, it is my job to be fair and impartial. The work we do supports the objectives and goals of the current Secretary of Energy and the President, no matter the administration. Therefore we must provide credible, objective analysis and reports that support the goals of the current administration - NREL prides itself on being impartial advisors to federal agencies, foreign governments, and state, county, and local governments. We present the data and analysis and avoid making policy recommendations - we let the readers of our analysis take away conclusions based on the quantitative and qualitative results. If we are asked to examine a particular policy, we strive to point out the relative benefits and challenges of that policy. If we are asked to compare different policies, we will point out the relative benefits and challenges with each one, yet we will NOT make a recommendation about which the best policy. Our main client is the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) and sometimes they come to us and are disappointed by our analysis results; we do not change the analysis results to match the current administration's hopes and dreams - we ensure that our analysis has solid footing and can withstand heavy scrutiny. Therefore, instead we walk the DOE client through the assumptions and calculation methodology - we explain why assumptions were made and the sources of those assumptions. In doing so, we gather agreement from the DOE client. Sometimes our DOE client will identify something that is surprising and offer an alternative input data set or approach. As an analyst, I will use active listening to make sure I understand what they are saying and consider whether their point is valid. If it makes sense and is generally supported with other sources, I will change the analysis and concede the point. Similarly, I believe strongly that honest communication among the commissioners will promote consensus and contribute to collaboration and agreement. Since I have worked 13 years at NREL, I have a lot of practice promoting consensus and contributing to creating agreement among career government officials and political appointees.

For example, I have already thought about how I think the Commission can be run efficiently - I am not totally wed to this idea, but I have run many stakeholder groups, and would recommend the following process based on my experience. I understand the data scientists would have access to the Census data and would draw a series of maps for consideration by the Commission and the public. I think it would be important to: 1) Hear a presentation by the data scientists on how they created the maps and key features of each. Allow time for Commissioners to ask questions during the presentation. 2) Be able to have at least a week to study their maps, including key demographic information for each of the maps (gender identify, political affiliation, total population, age,) - allow time for Commissioners to identify key questions (this allows introverts time to process the information and formulate questions) 4) Have one or more follow-up sessions with the data scientists where the Commission can ask more questions. 5) Schedule public sessions, 3 in each Congressional District, to hear feedback and field questions from the public. If there is a disagreement, capture both ideas. 7) I don't know if iteration with the data scientists on the map would be possible after the Commission reviews, or the general public reviews, but I would hope so.
