Background

Past Political Activity

In my teens I was past president of the Baylor Young Democrats, and I worked on my father's campaigns for City Council in Waco, Texas. I worked on various Democrats' campaigns in Colorado and Virginia as a volunteer. I have made small donations to various campaigns, most recently campaigns for Joe Biden, John Hickenlooper, Julian Castro, Amy McGrath, and Jaime Harrison. I also volunteered in efforts to elect School Board members in Jefferson County seven years ago to recall three right wing members.

I have walked with other volunteers in Jeffco neighborhoods for candidates of various offices in Colorado. During the recall of School Board members, I spoke in various meetings of volunteers and in school board meetings. Statement

I believe that elimination of gerrymandering would be one of the most important steps toward a more democratic election of national candidates. I would hope that this commission would work toward that end. My knowledge of political process and interest in improving it enables me to be a valuable contributor.

Statement on Working with Consensus

I was a teacher for 30 years and, before that, an advertising/marketing executive for 15 years. Creating consensus among my clients, peers, parents, and others was a skill that was sought. I also took coursework in consensus-building. I preferred to be a member rather than a leader on committees, but it was common that my thoughts would not only be considered by also valued as I tend to look at the bigger picture and not get caught up "in the weeds."

Relevant Analytic Skills for Commission

Critical thinking is a skill that must be developed over time. Over my work life that was a skill that was required for any level of success. As a teacher, one must evaluate constantly in the classroom and apply observation to action, constantly. It required more than simple evaluation of a student's work; it was seeing beyond that which was obvious. The most important thing as a teacher is to keep your eyes on the most important thing: students and learning. Everyday my "mantra" was, "If what I'm doing right now is not about students and learning, then I must be doing the wrong thing." As a teacher I became a reflective practitioner. I made a discovery that I passed on to my peers and others through my classroom research that I would like to share. It started early in my career when a 9th grade student in frustration threw her pencil to the floor and exclaimed, "I used to be able to spell." Indeed, she was a terrible speller. But I asked her when she could spell and when she couldn't. She said that she could spell in the 6th grade, but she could not spell in the 7th grade anymore. So, I asked what happened between the 6th and 7th grade, and she responded, with a slap to her forehead, "Oh, I was in a car accident and hit my head." It was then that I started asking questions of my students struggling with reading and writing about their academic histories. Invariably, there was some sort of head or hearing trauma that intervened in these students' learning. This was well before the attention on brain injuries in athletics. My Masters in Education prepared me to learn how to conduct research in a classroom setting. Bottom line, I discovered that by the time a student has reached his senior year, in a "non-advanced" English class at least 50% of students have had a head injury, and more boys than girls had experienced such an injury. I found that most boys who don't like to read have had an mTBI (Mild Traumatic Brain Injury). Most students who have been diagnosed with ADD or ADHD have had an mTBI. I don't mean to exclude girls; it's just that fewer have had an mTBI. I learned to look for ways to accommodate these conditions and that labels may be given to students who have difficulty reading or writing that are undeserved simply because a teacher has not asked, "What happened?" I have shared these realizations with others and met with district health personnel, and they get it. I also consulted with the Colorado Brain Injury Alliance who were also helpful in provided guidance and instruction. As an advertising/marketing executive I had to analyze a market's tendencies and use marketing research and focus groups to develop effective campaigns. My clients over the years included American Airlines, Hertz Rent-a-Car, Coors, and many others. A truth that was important to realize is that nothing will kill a bad product faster than good advertising. Another truth is that listening is much more important that talking. As a result, back in the early 80s I realized that was much more fit for teaching than for advertising. I realized what I was good at doing when I found myself teaching writing to the copywriters and critical thinking to my peers and supervisors. I also realized a truth about me: I valued ethical behavior and results. A famous ad exec once told me to never let the truth get between me and good advertising. I can never thank him enough for helping me realize something that was central to my being: the truth.
