Statement

I believe that voting is a sacred trust and protecting that trust is essential if our democracy is to survive and thrive. Chief Justice Earl Warren said “The basic principle of representative government remains, and must remain, unchanged – the weight of a citizen’s vote cannot be made to depend on where he lives.” While gerrymandering has been with us since the nation’s earliest days, this practice has intensified over the last 20 years to the point that over 90% of House of Representative seats are considered noncompetitive. Moreover, gerrymandering has been used to limit the electoral impact of ethnic communities. Gerrymandering continues to erode the public’s trust in the value of their vote and, as a result, has discouraged some from participating in the electoral process to the long-term detriment of our democracy. My desire to serve on the commission is to ensure that congressional districts are fairly drawn to ensure the equality of all votes and to promote the competition of ideas necessary to sustain the health of our democracy.

Statement on Working with Consensus

Bringing people together to achieve a common end has been foundational to my career success. People will always have differing views on or approaches to solving a problem or achieving an end. Consensus is satisfying but elusive. Agreement through informed compromise, however, is readily achievable. A key principle to achieving consensus or agreement is trust between team participants as well as with stakeholders. Developing trust requires a commitment to each other, respectfully listening to competing views, honestly considering these views, recognizing and setting aside our own biases, and persuasion. Whether a team leader or participant, I work to promote trust through dialogue, transparency, and patience to create an environment all will see as fair and impartial. Once trust is established compromise to achieve agreement is possible.

Relevant Analytic Skills for Commission

My training as a scientist and engineer ingrained in me the importance of objective, evidence-based data to analyze problems and create solutions. My training as a business executive taught me that the best decisions were not solely driven by hard, objective evidence, but required an understanding of the people or emotional elements. Taken together, my training has enabled me to not only use logic and reason to solve a problem, but to consider the human side of a problem to create a logically sound and emotionally satisfying solution.
